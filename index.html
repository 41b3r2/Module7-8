5 Pillars of Information Security
ConfidentialityPrivate/sensitive information seen only by authorized individuals. Controls: Access Control, Authentication, Encryption, Secure Auth Mechanisms, Network Security Controls, Physical Security, Administrative Controls
IntegrityInformation stays correct, complete, unchanged unless by authorized parties. Controls: Hashing, Digital Signatures, Access Control, Version Control, Logging & Audit Trails, Change Management
AvailabilitySystems/data accessible whenever authorized users need them. Controls: Redundancy, Failover Systems, Backup & Recovery, Regular Maintenance, Monitoring, Attack Protection, DR/BC
AuthenticityEnsures a person, system, or device is who they claim to be. Controls: Authentication Mechanisms, Digital Certificates, Biometrics, Cryptographic Keys, Device Authentication, Logging & Monitoring
Non-RepudiationA person cannot deny performing an action. Controls: Digital Signatures, Audit Trails, Logging & Monitoring, Timestamps, Strong Authentication, Secure Storage of Logs
📌 CIA = Confidentiality, Integrity, Availability — the core triad. Authenticity and Non-Repudiation extend it to 5 pillars.
Authentication, Authorization, Accountability
AuthenticationVerifies the identity of users, systems, or entities. Can prove identity of: User, Service/Process, Workstation/Server, Network Device. 3 Factors: Something you Know, Something you Have, Something you Are
AuthorizationGrants or denies rights/privileges after authentication. Models: RBAC (roles), ABAC (attributes), DAC (owner decides), MAC (policy-based security labels)
AccountabilityTraces actions back to the entity that performed them. Key components: Identification & Authentication, Logging & Monitoring, Access Controls, User Training & Awareness
Least PrivilegeUsers/systems/processes granted only minimum permissions needed to perform their function. Reduces attack surface and mitigates compromise impact
Need to KnowAccess limited to information strictly necessary for one's role/task, even if clearance level would allow broader access

BIA, DRP, and BCP
BIA — Business Impact AnalysisIdentifies and analyzes the impact of disruptions on business operations. Determines criticality of business functions/processes. Provides data that informs both DRP and BCP
DRP — Disaster Recovery PlanFocuses on recovery of IT systems and infrastructure. Key activities: identify critical IT assets, develop recovery strategies, establish IT recovery team
BCP — Business Continuity PlanBroader than DRP; covers all critical business functions, not just IT. Key activities: maintain/resume operations, identify alternative processes/locations, regular training and testing
📌 BIA informs both DRP and BCP. DRP = IT only. BCP = all business functions including IT.

Risk Management — Key Terms
AssetAny valuable resource owned by the organization. Tangible (computers, servers, buildings) or Intangible (software, IP, data, reputation)
VulnerabilityWeakness or gap in a security program that can be exploited. Can be technical (software bugs) or procedural (inadequate policies). Known or unknown
ThreatAny circumstance/event with potential to cause harm through unauthorized access, destruction, modification, or denial of service. Can be intentional or accidental
Threat AgentThe specific element/factor that materializes a threat. Can be non-human (flood, virus). Examples: flood, computer virus, disgruntled employee
Threat ActorIndividual/group responsible for intentionally exploiting vulnerabilities. Always involves intent. Typically human: hackers, insiders, competitors, state-sponsored groups
ExploitSpecific method/code used to take advantage of a vulnerability. Action-oriented; depends on vulnerability existence. Example: SQL injection exploit
RiskPotential for loss/damage when a threat exploits a vulnerability. Involves uncertainty. Risk = Threat × Vulnerability × Asset Value
ExposureExtent to which a system/asset is open to damage from a threat due to a vulnerability
CountermeasureSafeguard or mitigation implemented to detect, prevent, or minimize security risks. Also called controls
IncidentEvent that actually or potentially jeopardizes CIA of an information system. May or may not be malicious
BreachConfirmed incident where sensitive/protected/confidential data is accessed or disclosed in an unauthorized way
Quantitative Risk Analysis Metrics
AV — Asset ValueEstimated value of the asset being protected
EF — Exposure FactorPercentage of asset value lost when a specific threat successfully exploits a vulnerability
SLE — Single Loss ExpectancyExpected monetary loss each time a risk event occurs. Formula: SLE = AV × EF
ARO — Annualized Rate of OccurrenceEstimated frequency a threat occurs within a year
ALE — Annual Loss ExpectancyExpected monetary loss per year due to risks. Formula: ALE = SLE × ARO
📊 Example: AV=₱12M, EF=50% → SLE=₱6M. ARO=3 → ALE=₱18M

Data Destruction Methods (NIST SP 800-88r1)
DegausserHeavy magnetic field to obliterate data on magnetic media (HDDs, tapes, floppy disks)
ErasingDelete operation; data can be recovered unless overwrites occurred
Clearing (Overwriting)Sensitive data overwritten with non-sensitive data to make it unrecoverable
PurgingMore intense form of clearing; multiple clearing passes combined with another destruction method
DestructionDisintegrator, incineration, crushing, shredding, dissolving. For SSDs (degaussing doesn't work on SSDs)
DeclassificationPurging media so it can be reused in an unclassified environment; higher security environments destroy instead

Data in TransitData in motion (email, uploads, IMs, collaboration). Controls: TLS, encrypted protocols
Data in UseData being processed, read, or modified. Most vulnerable state. Controls: DRM/IRM, sandboxes

Asset Retention — EOL vs EOS
End of Life (EOL)Date when a vendor stops offering a product for sale; product still supported (warranties, updates, repairs, security patches)
End of Support (EOS / EOSL)Date when ALL vendor support ends — no more patches, updates, or fixes
⚠️ EOS/EOSL is more critical from a security standpoint — no more security patches means unpatched vulnerabilities forever.

Security Models
State MachineSystem has multiple states with transitions governed by defined rules and events; prevents unauthorized state changes
Multi-Level LatticeFocuses on confidentiality and information flow; upper bounds = most permissive; lower bounds = most restrictive
Matrix-BasedMapping of subjects and objects permissions (access control matrix)
Non-InterferenceActions of lower-level users/processes cannot interfere with higher-level information security or integrity
Information FlowBlocks information flow that violates security policy; The Pump prevents flow from lower to higher level
Take-GrantSubjects and objects with rights: TAKE (acquire permissions from another subject), GRANT (delegate permissions), CREATE (generate new subjects/objects), DELETE (remove subjects/objects)
Formal Security Models
Bell-LaPadulaPrimarily concerned with Confidentiality. MAC/State Machine. Levels: Unclassified → Restricted → Confidential → Secret → Top Secret. Simple Security Property: No Read Up. Star Property: No Write Down. Limitations: disregards integrity, poor covert channel handling, no need-to-know support
BibaConcerned with data Integrity. 3 levels: Low/Medium/High. No Write Up, No Read Down. Drawback: very strict
Clark-WilsonConcerned with data Integrity; prevents unauthorized and improper authorized modifications. Key: Separation of Duties, Well-Formed Transactions, Certification and Enforcement
LipnerCombines Bell-LaPadula and Biba; two implementation approaches: using BLP model only, or hybrid of BLP and BIBA
Brewer-Nash (Chinese Wall)Designed to mitigate conflicts of interest
Graham-DenningSecure creation and deletion of subjects and objects; 3 attributes: Subjects, Objects, Rules
Harrison-Ruzzo-UllmanVariation of Graham-Denning; maps subjects, objects, and access rights to an access matrix
Cryptography Basics
Core FeaturesConfidentiality, Integrity, Authentication (via Digital Signatures), Non-repudiation (via Digital Signatures)
Plain TextUnencrypted message
Cipher TextAn encrypted message
CipherThe encryption algorithm used to encrypt and decrypt
KeyDetermines the output of the cipher algorithm; needed to encrypt and decrypt
Symmetric EncryptionSingle key for both encryption and decryption. Fast for large data. Key exchange problem on first contact. Algorithms: DES (compromised), 3DES (resource-intensive), AES (current standard, US gov since 2002)
Asymmetric EncryptionKey pair: Public Key (shared) + Private Key (secret). Anything encrypted with Private Key → decrypted by Public Key, and vice versa. Also called PKI encryption
HashingOne-way function converting input to fixed-size string. Cannot reverse. Used for integrity verification. Common: MD5, SHA-1, SHA-256
Digital CertificateElectronic document associating an entity with a public key. Issued by Certificate Authority (CA). Contains: Serial Number, Signature Algorithm, Issuer, Valid-From, Valid-To, Public Key
Certificate Authority (CA)Trusted entity that verifies identity and issues/manages/signs digital certificates (e.g., VeriSign)

Malware Types
Buffer Overflow — writes beyond buffer into adjacent memory; allows malicious code injection
Viruses — needs host application and user execution; replicates on trigger
Worms — autonomous; no host app or user needed; consumes bandwidth
Trojan Horse — disguises as desirable software
Logic Bomb — triggers on specific event (date, program launch)
Spyware — silently collects keystrokes, screenshots, credentials, PII
Ransomware — encrypts files; demands payment for decryption
Adware — shows unwanted ads; collects marketing data
Rootkit — gains root access via privilege escalation; invisible to OS
Backdoor — alternative entry point bypassing security controls
Zero Day — exploits unknown flaws with no patch on day of discovery
Attacks & Social Engineering
Social EngineeringBypasses technology by manipulating people; mitigations: training, identity verification, suspicion
PhishingDeceptive email to reveal personal info or click malicious links
Email SpoofingForged email header; appears from legitimate source; used in phishing/spam
DoS / DDoSFloods server with requests; DDoS uses many machines. Types: Buffer Overflow, SYN Attack, Ping of Death
Replay AttackCaptures and resends valid auth message to create trusted relationship
SQL InjectionMalicious SQL via web forms; can retrieve, delete, drop tables
XSSMalicious HTML/JS in website; executes on victim's browser; steals cookies/session data
RCERemote Code Execution; runs system-level code via improperly coded web apps
Weak EncryptionEnough traffic captured to break key (e.g., WEP)
Malware Breakdown (Module 8 Focus)
Virus TypesBoot Sector, Program, Script, Macro, and Polymorphic viruses. Usually require host execution, then replicate and trigger payload
Worm BehaviorSelf-propagates through networks without user action; rapidly consumes bandwidth and can spread to many hosts
Rootkit RiskDesigned for stealthy persistence and privilege escalation; can modify core OS components to evade detection
Spyware vs AdwareSpyware steals data quietly (keystrokes, screenshots, credentials). Adware mainly pushes unwanted ads and tracking
Zero-Day vs N-DayZero-Day has no available patch at discovery; N-Day targets already disclosed vulnerabilities, often unpatched in practice
Backdoor & Logic BombBackdoor bypasses normal controls; logic bomb triggers malicious action on date/event/condition
Protecting Against Malware & Attacks
Antivirus / Anti-SpywareDetects common malware families (virus, trojan, worm, rootkit, spyware, adware) using signatures + behavior analysis
Patch ManagementFrequent updates reduce exploitability of software vulnerabilities (especially RCE and injection vectors)
Secure Coding + ValidationInput validation, output encoding, and safe query handling reduce SQL injection, XSS, and command injection risks
Hardening & Least PrivilegeRestrict services, enforce MFA, reduce admin rights, and disable unnecessary ports/features to limit blast radius
User AwarenessTrain users to detect phishing, spoofed emails, suspicious links/attachments, and social engineering tactics
Threat IntelligenceFeeds known IOCs, fake domains, malware variants, and C2 patterns into detection tools for proactive defense
